# Raha's Math Curriculum

**Age:** 5 years old
**Goal:** Build genuine number intuition — not rote memorization, not speed, not flashcards. A child who understands what 37 means is years ahead of a child who can recite it.
**Pedagogy:** CPA (Concrete → Pictorial → Abstract). Singapore Math sequence. Jo Boaler's number sense principles. No timers. No wrong-answer punishment.
**Session length:** 15 minutes/day. Short and consistent beats long and occasional.

---

## Guiding Principles

1. **Concrete before abstract.** She touches/drags objects before she sees a numeral. Always.
2. **No timers.** Speed pressure is the #1 cause of math anxiety. Never introduce time as a variable.
3. **Mistakes are thinking.** Wrong answers get a gentle prompt, never a sting. "Hmm, let me look again..." not "Wrong!"
4. **Visual patterns over counting.** A child who sees 7 as "a full five and two more" thinks faster than one who counts from 1 every time.
5. **Talk about math.** Say "3 groups of 4" out loud. Language builds the concept.
6. **Stop before boredom.** 15 minutes of genuine engagement > 45 minutes of drifting attention.

## Stage Design Rules Learned From Phase 1

These rules apply to every later phase in the game implementation.

1. **Recognition before recall.** Introduce a new concept with the answer still visually supported on screen. Only fade supports after she is confidently matching the meaning.
2. **Do not accidentally test working memory.** If the concept is quantity, grouping, place value, or bonds, the task should not depend on remembering a vanished picture unless memory is the explicit skill being trained.
3. **One new abstraction at a time.** Do not combine a new math concept with a new interaction pattern, heavy reading demand, or extra audio complexity in the same moment.
4. **Speech supports; it does not carry the task.** Every important state change must still be understandable through visuals, motion, and interaction even if the spoken line is ignored.
5. **Words stay visible, but meaning cannot depend on reading.** Keep written language on screen for literacy exposure, but pair it with non-reading cues so the child can still act immediately.
6. **Scaffolds fade, not disappear.** Move from concrete + pictorial + numeral, to reduced support, to numeral-only or symbol-only only after the earlier representation is stable.
7. **Reduce irrelevant difficulty.** Do not let the stage become a test of remembering instructions, interpreting hidden UI, or tolerating robotic speech instead of the actual math idea.

## Phase 1 — Subitizing & Counting to 10
**Weeks 1–4**

### What she'll learn
- Instantly recognize quantities 1-5 without counting (subitizing)
- Count objects 1-10 with one-to-one correspondence
- Understand cardinality: the last number you say IS the total
- Compare: more, fewer, same

### Why this phase matters
Subitizing is the foundation of all number sense. Kids who can't instantly see "5" will count from 1 every single time for years. This phase builds the visual shortcut that makes everything else faster.

### Daily activities

**Game: Dot Flash (5 min)**
Show a card with 1-5 dots for 2 seconds. "How many?" No counting — just look. Start with 1-3, add 4-5 after she's confident. Use dice, dot cards, or the game.

**Game: Counting Objects (5 min)**
Count real things: grapes, blocks, fingers, stairs. Touch each one. Say the number. When done: "So how many grapes?" (She should say the last number, not recount — this is cardinality.)

**Game: More or Fewer? (5 min)**
Two groups of objects. "Which has more?" Then count to check. Builds estimation before precision.

### How to know she's ready to move on
- Sees 5 dots and says "5" without counting — immediately
- Counts 10 objects without skipping or double-counting
- Answers "how many?" with the last counting number (not a recount)

## Phase 2 — Ten-Frames & Numbers to 20
**Weeks 5–8**

### What she'll learn
- Use a ten-frame (2×5 grid) to visualize numbers to 10, then to 20
- Recognize numbers 11-20 as "ten and some more"
- Count to 20 reliably
- See 7 as "5 and 2" — not just the number after 6

### Why this phase matters
The ten-frame is the single most powerful tool in early math. It anchors every number to 5 and 10 — the building blocks of our entire number system. A child who sees 8 as "a full five-row plus 3 more" has the foundation for addition, subtraction, and eventually multiplication.

### Daily activities

**Game: Fill the Frame (5 min)**
Draw or print a ten-frame (2 rows of 5 boxes). Roll a die. Fill that many squares with objects or marks. "How many filled? How many empty?" Repeat.

**Game: Ten-Frame Flash (5 min)**
Show a filled ten-frame for 2 seconds. "How many?" She should start seeing patterns: full top row (5) + some on bottom.

**Game: Teen Numbers (5 min)**
Use two ten-frames. Fill the first completely (10). Add some to the second. "One full frame is 10. Plus 4 more — that's 14." Build each teen number this way.

### How to know she's ready to move on
- Fills a ten-frame to any number without placing wrong count
- Says "13 is ten and three more" without prompting
- Counts to 20 reliably, including the teens (which are tricky — "eleven, twelve" break the pattern)

**Implementation note:** Early ten-frame tasks should keep the frame visible while she answers. Do not hide the representation and force a memory jump to the numeral before the 5-and-10 structure is stable.

## Phase 3 — Number Bonds to 10
**Weeks 9–12**

### What she'll learn
- All pairs of numbers that add to 5: (0+5, 1+4, 2+3, 3+2, 4+1, 5+0)
- All pairs that add to 10: (0+10, 1+9, 2+8... 5+5... 10+0)
- Part-part-whole thinking: a number is made of two parts
- "Missing part" problems: 10 = 6 + ?

### Why this phase matters
Number bonds to 10 are the most important math fact a young child can learn. Every addition and subtraction to 100 — and eventually every multiplication shortcut — flows from knowing these pairs cold. This is not memorization; it's visual pattern recognition built from the ten-frame.

### Daily activities

**Game: Ten-Frame Bonds (5 min)**
Put 7 counters on a ten-frame. "How many filled? How many empty?" 7 filled, 3 empty. "So 7 and 3 make 10." Write it: 7 + 3 = 10. Do all pairs.

**Game: Missing Part (5 min)**
"I have 10 strawberries. I hid some. Here are 4. How many did I hide?" She finds the missing part. Use objects first, then draw, then just talk.

**Game: Bond War (5 min)**
Each player draws a number card (1-9). First to say "and I need ____ more to make 10!" wins the point. Fun, no timer, turn-based.

### How to know she's ready to move on
- Says "7 and 3" immediately when you say "make 10 with 7" — no counting on fingers
- Solves "10 = 4 + ?" with objects in under 20 seconds
- Can do number bonds to 5 without objects

**Implementation note:** Number bonds should start with visible part-part-whole support. The missing part can be inferred from the picture before it is expected from the symbol alone.

## Phase 4 — Addition & Subtraction to 20
**Weeks 13–18**

### What she'll learn
- Addition as "combining groups" (concrete → picture → symbol)
- Subtraction as "taking away" and as "the difference between"
- + and = as symbols only after she understands the concept with objects
- Strategies: counting on, using ten-frame, using number bonds

### Why this phase matters
Most curricula rush to symbols. A child who writes "4 + 3 = 7" without a concrete picture in her head is doing symbol manipulation, not math. The sequence here: objects first, pictures second, symbols third — and only after the first two are solid.

### Daily activities

**Week 13-14: Addition with objects**
Two bowls. 4 apples in one, 3 in the other. Push together. Count total. "We combined 4 and 3 and got 7." No + signs yet.

**Week 15-16: Addition with ten-frames**
Show two ten-frames side by side. 6 dots on one, 5 on the other. "Can we move some to fill the first frame?" Move 4 from the second frame to fill the first: now 10 + 1. "6 + 5 = 11." She sees why.

**Week 17-18: Introduce symbols + subtraction**
Now write: 6 + 5 = 11. Show the symbol matches what she already knows. Subtraction: "7 apples, eat 3. How many left?" Remove objects. Then: 7 - 3 = 4.

### How to know she's ready to move on
- Adds any two numbers to 20 using objects or a ten-frame
- Understands subtraction as removal and as difference
- Can write a number sentence to match a story

**Implementation note:** Addition and subtraction should not jump straight from objects to bare numerals. Keep the bowls, frames, or moved objects visible while the symbol is introduced.

## Phase 5 — Place Value & Counting to 100
**Weeks 19–24**

### What she'll learn
- Tens and ones: 37 = 3 tens + 7 ones
- Count to 100 by understanding the structure (not rote recitation)
- Build numbers with bundled tens + loose ones
- Read and write numbers to 100

### Why this phase matters
Rote counting to 100 is a party trick. Understanding that 47 is "4 groups of ten and 7 more" is place value — the foundation of all multi-digit arithmetic and eventually long multiplication and division.

### Daily activities

**Game: Bundling (5 min)**
Count out objects. When you get to 10, bundle them with a rubber band or put in a cup. "One bundle of ten. Then we keep going." Build numbers as bundles + loose.

**Game: Tens/Ones Mat (5 min)**
Draw two columns: TENS | ONES. Use bundles of 10 sticks and single sticks. Show a number. She builds it. Then reads it. "3 bundles and 5 sticks = 35."

**Game: What's My Number? (5 min)**
"I have 4 tens and 2 ones. What's my number?" She answers: 42. Reverse: "What's 68?" She shows 6 bundles + 8 singles.

### How to know she's ready to move on
- Builds any number to 100 with tens/ones materials
- Says "47 is 4 tens and 7 ones" without prompting
- Counts to 100 without stalling at the decade transitions (29→30, 39→40)

**Implementation note:** Place value should keep the bundles and loose ones visible next to the numeral. Do not ask for the numeral alone until "3 tens and 7 ones" is clearly understood.

## Phase 6 — Skip Counting
**Weeks 25–28**

### What she'll learn
- Count by 2s to 20 (2, 4, 6, 8, 10...)
- Count by 5s to 50 (5, 10, 15, 20...)
- Count by 10s to 100 (10, 20, 30...)
- See the patterns in a hundred chart

### Why this phase matters
Skip counting is the bridge to multiplication. "3 groups of 4" is just skip counting by 4, three times. It also builds pattern recognition — the most transferable math skill there is.

### Daily activities

**Count by 2s with body:** clap on every other step going upstairs. Count aloud: 2, 4, 6...

**Count by 5s with a clock:** the minute hand lands on 5-minute marks. Count the marks: 5, 10, 15...

**Hundred chart:** color every 10th square. Color every 5th. See the grid patterns.

**Song/rhythm:** skip count to a rhythm. 2s and 5s especially become automatic through repetition with a beat.

### How to know she's ready to move on
- Counts by 10s to 100 without pausing
- Counts by 5s to 50 fluently
- Counts by 2s to 20 fluently

**Implementation note:** Skip counting should lean on rhythm, grouping, and repeated visual spacing. Avoid making it a pure audio recitation task.

## Phase 7 — Multiplication as "Groups Of"
**Weeks 29–36**

### What she'll learn
- Multiplication means "equal groups"
- 3 × 4 means "3 groups of 4" — always start with words, not symbols
- Arrays: rows and columns of dots
- Connection between arrays and skip counting
- Simple facts through pattern, not memorization

### Why this phase matters
Children taught multiplication as a table to memorize before understanding it conceptually develop math anxiety and fragile knowledge. Children who first build arrays and connect them to skip counting build a mental model that makes every multiplication fact derivable — even the ones they haven't memorized.

### Daily activities

**Week 29-31: Equal groups**
"Set the table for 3 friends. Each friend gets 4 strawberries. Make the groups." Physical objects. Count the total. "3 groups of 4 is 12." No × symbol yet.

**Week 32-34: Arrays**
Arrange objects in rows and columns. "3 rows of 4 dots." Count total. Draw it. "Can you see the skip counting? 4, 8, 12." Connect array to skip count.

**Week 35-36: Introduce × symbol**
"You already know that 3 groups of 4 is 12. That's what 3 × 4 means." The symbol is just shorthand for what she already understands. Practice reading arrays as multiplication sentences.

### How to know she's completed the curriculum
- Makes "groups of" with objects for any small multiplication
- Draws an array and writes the multiplication sentence
- Counts by 2s, 5s, 10s and connects to multiplication (2×3 = skip count by 2, three times)
- Understands that multiplication is commutative because you can rotate an array (3×4 = 4×3, same dots different orientation)

**Implementation note:** Multiplication should begin with visible groups and arrays on screen. The child should be able to see the structure while hearing the language before the `×` symbol is asked to carry meaning by itself.

## What to Avoid

| Don't | Why |
|---|---|
| Timed drills or flashcard races | Creates anxiety, kills intuition |
| Jumping to symbols before objects | Child memorizes procedures without understanding |
| Praising speed ("you're so fast!") | Tells her speed is what math is about |
| Rote counting to 100 before place value | She'll say the words without understanding the structure |
| Multiplication tables before groups/arrays | Facts without meaning, forgotten quickly |
| "That's wrong" without a visual | Shame without redirection |

## Year 2 — Ages 6–7 (Phases 8–12)

After Phase 7, she has solid number intuition to 100, can add/subtract to 20, understands place value, skip counts fluently, and sees multiplication as equal groups. The next year builds on every one of those foundations.

| Phase | Concept | Timeline | Key Milestone |
|---|---|---|---|
| 8 | Multi-digit addition & subtraction | Weeks 37-44 | Adds/subtracts two 2-digit numbers with understanding (not tricks) |
| 9 | Multiplication fluency | Weeks 45-52 | Times tables 2s, 5s, 10s; area model for larger facts |
| 10 | Division as sharing & grouping | Weeks 53-60 | Solves 12÷3 two ways: sharing and grouping |
| 11 | Fractions as equal parts | Weeks 61-68 | Names ½, ¼, ⅓ and finds them in real objects |
| 12 | Measurement & time | Weeks 69-76 | Reads clocks to half-hour, compares lengths and weights |

### Phase 8 — Multi-Digit Addition & Subtraction
**Weeks 37–44**

### What she'll learn
- Add two 2-digit numbers (23 + 45, then 37 + 48 with regrouping)
- Subtract two 2-digit numbers (with and without borrowing)
- **Regrouping as a concept, not a trick:** when ones go over 10, a new ten is born
- Mental math strategies: round to nearest 10, then adjust

### Why this phase matters
Most children are taught "carry the 1" as a procedure without understanding why. A child who built place value with physical tens-bundles understands that "carrying" just means trading 10 loose ones for a new bundle. The procedure flows from the concept.

### Daily activities

**Concrete first:** 37 + 48 with sticks. Count ones: 7+8=15. Trade 10 loose sticks for a bundle. Now you have 1 extra bundle + 5 loose, plus the original 3+4 bundles. 8 bundles + 5 loose = 85.

**Pictorial:** draw tens/ones columns. No algorithm yet — just draw and count.

**Abstract (Week 43-44):** now write the column addition algorithm. She sees it's describing what she's been doing all along.

**Subtraction with regrouping:** "I need to take 8 from 3. I don't have enough ones. Open a bundle." Physical bundles first, then pictures, then algorithm.

### How to know she's ready to move on
- Adds any two 2-digit numbers including those that require regrouping
- Explains WHY you "carry the 1" (it's a new ten-bundle)
- Subtracts with regrouping using pictures if not yet in head

### Phase 9 — Multiplication Fluency
**Weeks 45–52**

### What she'll learn
- Times tables for 2s, 5s, 10s (connected to skip counting she already knows)
- Times tables for 3s, 4s through arrays and area models
- Commutativity: why 3×4 = 4×3 (rotate the array)
- Area model: 6×7 = (6×5) + (6×2) — breaking facts into easier parts

### Why this phase matters
She already knows multiplication as "groups of." Now she builds fluency — but through patterns and derivation, not brute memorization. A child who forgets 7×8 should be able to derive it (7×8 = 7×4×2 = 28×2 = 56), not be stuck.

### Key insight: never memorize without an anchor
- 2s = skip counting she already knows
- 5s = skip counting she already knows
- 10s = place value she already knows
- 4s = double the 2s
- 3s = build arrays, find the pattern
- 6s = double the 3s
- 9s = one less than 10s (9×7 = 10×7 - 7 = 63)
- 7s, 8s last — by then she can derive anything she forgets

### Daily activities

**Area model:** 6×7 is a rectangle, 6 rows of 7. Split into 6×5 (she knows this: 30) + 6×2 (she knows: 12). Total: 42. She can always derive.

**Array rotation:** make a 3×4 array of stickers. Rotate the paper. It's now 4×3. Same stickers. Commutativity is visual.

### Phase 10 — Division as Sharing & Grouping
**Weeks 53–60**

### What she'll learn
- Division as equal sharing: 12 ÷ 3 = "share 12 among 3 friends, how many each?"
- Division as grouping: 12 ÷ 3 = "make groups of 3 from 12, how many groups?"
- Division as the inverse of multiplication (if 3×4=12, then 12÷3=4)
- Remainders: what happens when it doesn't divide evenly

### Why this phase matters
Two completely different mental models of division — sharing and grouping — are both valid and both needed. Children taught only one get confused when the other appears in word problems. Connecting division to multiplication (fact families) builds the most durable knowledge.

### Daily activities

**Sharing:** 15 grapes, 3 friends. Deal them out one at a time. How many each?

**Grouping:** 15 grapes. Make bags of 5. How many bags?

**Fact families:** 3 × 5 = 15, 5 × 3 = 15, 15 ÷ 3 = 5, 15 ÷ 5 = 3. Four facts, one family. One array.

---

### Phase 11 — Fractions as Equal Parts
**Weeks 61–68**

### What she'll learn
- A fraction names equal parts of a whole: ½, ¼, ⅓
- The bottom number = how many equal parts total; the top = how many we have
- Fractions of a set: ½ of 8 apples = 4
- Comparing fractions: why ½ > ¼ (fewer cuts = bigger pieces)

### Why this phase matters
Fractions are where many children permanently fall behind in math. The reason is almost always the same: symbols introduced before concept. A child who has physically cut paper into thirds and shared 12 objects among 4 friends owns fractions. A child taught "top number divided by bottom number" is guessing.

### Daily activities

**Fold paper:** fold in half, in quarters, in thirds. See that more folds = smaller pieces. Name the parts.

**Fraction of a set:** "You have 12 strawberries. Give me ¼." She deals into 4 equal groups, takes 1 group: 3.

**Compare:** hold up ½ of a big piece of paper vs ¼ of the same paper. Which is more? Why?

---

### Phase 12 — Measurement & Time
**Weeks 69–76**

### What she'll learn
- Measure length with a ruler (cm and inches)
- Compare weights: heavier/lighter, estimate then check
- Read analog clocks: hour, then half-hour, then five-minute intervals
- Count coins (pennies, nickels, dimes, quarters)

### Why this phase matters
Measurement grounds abstract numbers in the physical world. A child who knows that 30 cm is "about the length of her forearm" has a number sense anchor that lasts. Telling time is the first real-world application of skip counting by 5s.

### Daily activities

**Time:** clock with movable hands. Set it, read it, tell a story about it. "It's 3:30 — what might we be doing?"

**Measurement:** measure everything around the house. Compare. "The book is 24 cm. The tablet is 19 cm. How much longer is the book?"

**Coins:** play store. Price things in round numbers. Make change.
